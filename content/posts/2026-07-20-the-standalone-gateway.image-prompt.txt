SURFACE:
CloakCode Part 2 blog hero and social preview. Visually consistent with the Part 1 hero
(content/assets/your-copilot-is-waiting-hero.jpg) — same editorial flat-vector style and palette —
while telling Part 2's own story.

STORY:
Several VS Code windows spread across two machines each publish their own phone link and QR code,
turning the phone into a wallet of QR codes. The CloakCode gateway multiplexes all of them into a
single phone link — and the moment that hub is exposed beyond loopback it locks itself behind a
6-digit authenticator code.

IMAGE PROMPT:
A bold editorial vector illustration for a technology blog banner, 16:9 landscape composition.

On the left third, a loose cluster of three or four screens — a mix of laptops and desktop monitors
representing several VS Code windows spread across two machines. Each screen shows an abstract
coding-agent interface and emits its own small stylized QR-code glyph: an abstract square-dot
pattern, clearly a QR-like tile rather than a real scannable code. Several thin, slightly tangled
connection lines fan out from these screens — the sprawling wallet of QR codes.

Across the center, all of those thin lines converge and collapse into a single central hub: a clean
rounded-rectangle node that merges many inputs into one, with a small padlock badge on it to show it
secures itself. Many thin lines flow in from the left; exactly one bold, confident line leaves toward
the right.

On the right third, one oversized smartphone. Its screen shows a single connection and, front and
center, a prominent padlock sitting above a row of six small empty rounded squares — an abstract
passcode field standing in for a 6-digit authenticator code, with no numerals filled in. The phone
reads as gated: the single link is reachable only past the lock.

The story should be instantly understandable at thumbnail size: many scattered screens and QR codes
on the left collapse through one hub in the middle into a single, code-locked phone link on the right.

Style: sophisticated flat vector editorial illustration, modern technology magazine artwork, bold
geometric shapes, crisp outlines, screen-print or risograph influence, strong visual hierarchy,
minimal details, intentional negative space, clean and graphic rather than photorealistic or 3D.

Color palette: warm off-white background, near-black outlines, coral red, mint green, cyan, and a
small amount of golden yellow. High contrast but not neon. No gradients. Use cyan and mint for the
converging connection lines and the central hub; reserve the golden yellow and coral for the padlock
and the six-square passcode field so the security lock is the clear focal accent.

Composition: cluster of small screens with QR glyphs on the left, thin lines converging through the
central hub, and one oversized code-locked smartphone on the right. Leave generous uncluttered space
near the upper center for an optional headline.

No words, no readable text, no numerals or legible digits (the six passcode squares stay empty), no
logos, no GitHub or Copilot branding, no watermark, no real scannable QR codes, no person, no face,
no hands. Exactly one central hub and exactly one smartphone; the left cluster may contain several
screens.
